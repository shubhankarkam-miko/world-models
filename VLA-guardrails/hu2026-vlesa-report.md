# VLESA: Vision-Language Embodied Safety Agent for Human Activity Monitoring

## TL;DR
VLESA turns a vision-language model into a real-time bystander safety monitor: watching egocentric (first-person) video, it infers what the wearer is trying to do, predicts a few candidate next actions, and scores each one for safety *conditioned on that inferred goal* — because the same action (e.g. picking up a knife) is safe or dangerous depending on intent, not on the action alone. The safety scoring is done by a small VLM (Qwen3-VL-2B) fine-tuned with GRPO (a reinforcement-learning post-training method) on a new synthetic dataset, EgoSafety, built by having a larger VLM generate plausible "unsafe variants" of real Ego4D action annotations. On the ASIMOV-2.0-Video benchmark, this goal-conditioned, RL-trained filter more than doubles intervention accuracy over a same-architecture prompting baseline (67.2% vs 28.0% at the exact ground-truth moment) and beats every frontier foundation model tested (GPT-5, Claude, Gemini) on the accuracy/timeliness Pareto front, while also improving the safety of the action it ultimately selects by ~41 percentage points via constrained decoding.

## Architecture

VLESA is a **three-stage pipeline** built by composing existing VLMs with a purpose-trained safety classifier — it is not a new end-to-end network, but the way the pieces are wired together (particularly the explicit goal-conditioning) is the paper's central design idea.

**Stage 1 — Intent–Action Prediction Agent.** Given a stream of egocentric frames `I_1:t`, a frozen, off-the-shelf VLM (Llama-4-Scout-17B-16E-Instruct by default) is shown a small set of adaptively-selected keyframes and asked to jointly (a) infer the latent task goal `ĝ` the camera wearer is pursuing, and (b) propose `K` candidate next actions, each expressed as a scene-graph triplet `(subject, predicate, object)` in a constrained vocabulary (`219` verbs, `407` objects, `16` relations) borrowed from the Ego4D/EASG annotation schema, then converted to a natural-language sentence via deterministic grammar rules. This factorization is written formally as `P(ĝ, a_{t+1} | I_1:t) = P(ĝ | I_1:t) · P(a_{t+1} | I_1:t, ĝ)` (Eq. 1) — the key point being that the inferred goal `ĝ` is exposed as an explicit variable that downstream safety scoring can condition on, rather than being folded invisibly into the action prediction.

**Stage 2 — Goal-Conditioned Safety Q-Filter.** A second, much smaller VLM (Qwen3-VL-2B-Instruct) is fine-tuned to answer a visual question-answering task: given an image `I`, the inferred goal `g`, and a candidate action sentence `a`, output "Safe" or "Unsafe" with chain-of-thought reasoning. Its output is mapped to a scalar `Q_φ(I,a,g) ∈ {-1, +1}` (Eq. 5), following the sign convention of [Control Barrier Functions](#background-borrowed-concepts): negative means safe, positive means unsafe, and the zero level set is the safety boundary (Eq. 3). The paper positions this `Q_φ` design as following "Q-based safety filters from control," a lineage rooted in [Hamilton-Jacobi reachability](#background-borrowed-concepts)-based safety analysis. The paper is explicit that this is philosophically different from a standard RL value function `Q^π(s,a)`, which is tied to one fixed policy/task — because `g` is an explicit input rather than baked into the model's training distribution, the *same* trained `Q_φ` can be paired with any intent-inference system (video-inferred, user-typed, or externally supplied) without retraining.

**Stage 3 — Constrained Decoding.** Each of the `K` candidate actions gets a safety score `s_k = Q_φ(I_t, a_k, ĝ)`, combined with the upstream VLM's own confidence ranking into `Score(a_k) = (1 - k/K) + α·(-s_k)` (α=2.0 by default). Let `S` be the subset of candidates with `s_k < τ` (τ=0, the safe/unsafe boundary). If `S` is non-empty, the system outputs the highest-`Score` candidate from `S` and raises a "safe" alert; if every candidate is judged unsafe (`S = ∅`), it falls back to the *least* unsafe candidate and raises a "danger" alert (Eq. 6, Algorithm 1). This is the mechanism that turns a classifier into an actionable intervention system: it doesn't just flag danger, it also tries to suggest the safest available alternative action.

**Training procedure for the Q-filter (GRPO).** The Q-filter is trained with Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) — the RL post-training method introduced for DeepSeekMath and used in DeepSeek-R1 — rather than supervised fine-tuning. For each training prompt (image + goal + action), the model samples a group of `G=4` responses, assigns a binary reward (+1 correct label, −1 incorrect), computes a *group-relative* advantage `A(y_i) = r(y_i) - mean(r(y_1..G))` to reduce variance without needing a separate learned critic, and optimizes a PPO-style clipped surrogate objective with a KL penalty back to the frozen reference model (Eq. 4). This is a lightweight RL recipe (30 training steps, learning rate 1e-5, KL coefficient β=0.01) rather than a large-scale RLHF run — notable given the small base model (2B parameters).

## Data

- **EgoSafety (the paper's new dataset)**, built on **Ego4D** (a large existing egocentric video corpus) with **Egocentric Action Scene Graph (EASG)** annotations, which ground each action to a pre-action, point-of-no-return, and post-action frame plus a symbolic scene graph.
- **Safe examples**: taken directly from the existing, real EASG annotation of what the camera wearer actually did — no generation needed.
- **Unsafe examples**: this is the paper's key data-generation trick. Rather than expensively rolling out unsafe video (physically or through video generation), they keep the *image frame and goal fixed* and prompt a VLM (Llama-4-Scout) to produce a minimally-edited, contextually-plausible *unsafe variant of the scene-graph triplets* — e.g., changing "inserts plug into outlet" to something that violates a safety rule given that same frame and goal. This reframes unsafe-data generation from an expensive video/trajectory rollout problem into a cheap graph-editing + VQA-labeling problem.
- **Safety specification**: a **32-rule "[robot constitution](#background-borrowed-concepts)"** (borrowed from Sermanet et al. 2025, reproduced in full in Appendix A) covering harm prevention, hazard awareness, contamination avoidance, communication, and resource management — this is what the unsafe-generation VLM and the safety labels are checked against, rather than a hand-built list of unsafe actions.
- **Vocabulary control**: generated triplets are constrained to, and validated against, the same closed vocabularies as the source EASG data (219 verbs / 407 objects / 16 relations), with substring-matching fallback and discarding of triplets that fail validation — meant to keep generated unsafe actions inside the same semantic space the source dataset covers (i.e., not just injecting nonsense).
- **Evaluation benchmarks**: (1) **ASIMOV-2.0-Video** (Jindal et al. 2025, external benchmark) — 287 short (5–10s) photorealistic videos generated with VEO3, each grounded in a real NEISS (National Electronic Injury Surveillance System) injury narrative, annotated by 5 human raters for the ground-truth intervention timestamp; after applying the official consensus filter (60% agreement, σ<1.0s) 189 videos remain with valid labels. (2) **EgoSafety's own held-out test split**, a balanced Safe/Unsafe binary classification set, used to isolate the Q-filter's intrinsic classification quality from upstream intent-prediction noise.

## Training setup

- **Q-filter backbone**: Qwen3-VL-2B-Instruct, images at 640×360, flash-attention for efficiency.
- **GRPO hyperparameters**: group size G=4, KL coefficient β=0.01, learning rate 1e-5, 30 training steps, bfloat16 precision.
- **Inference-time sampling for the Q-filter**: temperature T=0.6, nucleus sampling p=0.95, up to 1024 tokens of chain-of-thought reasoning, with the classification parsed from a `\boxed{}` tag (falling back to keyword search).
- **Intent–action predictor**: Llama-4-Scout-17B-16E-Instruct-FP8 by default (Llama-4-Maverick-17B-128E-Instruct tested in an ablation), temperature T=0.7, up to 2048 tokens, K ∈ {1,3,5} candidate actions.
- **Keyframe selection**: up to N=8 keyframes; three strategies implemented (uniform, recency-biased, motion-based adaptive) — uniform sampling is reported to give the best efficiency/coverage trade-off and is used throughout.
- **Constrained decoding parameters**: safety weight α=2.0, decision threshold τ=0 (the natural Q-filter boundary) for the main results; note Table's implementation section separately lists τ=0.5 for one experiment condition — the paper isn't fully consistent about this value between the main text (τ=0) and the implementation paragraph (τ=0.5), which is worth flagging as a minor inconsistency rather than a deliberate choice explained anywhere.
- **Real-time deployment details**: a sliding frame buffer of size 2N, continuously-updated goal tracking, and a three-level alert scheme based on raw Q-value thresholds (below −0.3 = "safe," −0.3 to 0.1 = "warning," above 0.1 = "danger").

## Evaluation & results

Two headline metrics: **Intervention Accuracy** (did the system flag danger within a time window `Δt` of the human-annotated ground-truth moment?) and **Post-Filter Safe Rate (SR)** (of the videos where an intervention was triggered, what fraction of the *finally selected* action is itself judged safe after constrained decoding?).

- **Main Pareto-front comparison (Figure 3, ASIMOV-2.0-Video, K=1)**: VLESA dominates a same-architecture "prompt-based" baseline (same Llama-4-Scout backbone and pipeline, but using zero-shot prompting instead of the GRPO-trained Q-filter) at every time tolerance: 43% vs 19% at the exact ground-truth frame (Δt=0), 72% vs 40% at Δt≤0.5s, 81% vs 47% at Δt≤1.0s. Notably, VLESA at Δt≤1.0s (81%) already beats what the prompting baseline achieves even at a much looser Δt≤3.0s (66%). VLESA also dominates the Pareto front against 8 frontier foundation models evaluated under the official ASIMOV-2.0 protocol (GPT-5, GPT-5-Mini/Nano, Claude Opus 4.1, Claude Sonnet 4, Gemini 2.5 Pro/Flash/Flash-Lite) — even the prompting baseline, just by virtue of using the structured goal+action-level decomposition rather than holistic scene classification, is competitive with these closed frontier models.
- **Effect of K (Table 3, Figure 5, Appendix C.2)**: increasing the number of candidate actions screened per timestep monotonically helps both methods (an intervention fires if *any* candidate is judged unsafe), but VLESA extracts far more value from it: K=1→3 raises VLESA's Δt=0 accuracy from 41.8% to 67.2%, while K=3→5 only adds ~3 more points (diminishing returns); even VLESA's weakest setting (K=1, 43.4%) beats the prompting baseline's strongest setting (K=5, 30.7%) at Δt=0. By K=3, VLESA's Pareto front nearly saturates (95.8% at Δt≤0.5s, ≥98% for Δt≥1.0s), while the prompting baseline needs both larger K *and* a much looser time tolerance (Δt≤3.0s) to approach VLESA's accuracy — the paper's interpretation is that the baseline's successful interventions are "systematically late rather than timely."
- **Constrained decoding / Safe Rate (Table 1)**: both methods gain a similar ~41 points of Safe Rate from re-ranking (pre-filter → post-filter: prompting 48.9%→89.4%, VLESA 37.3%→78.6%). But the paper argues the prompting baseline's higher absolute post-filter number is a misleading artifact of a *bias toward predicting "Safe"* (95.0% safe recall vs only 35.1% unsafe recall on the EgoSafety test set — Table 2), which inflates its safety score while causing it to actually miss real hazards, which is why its raw intervention accuracy is so much lower (28.0% vs 67.2% at Δt=0, K=3 — Table 3). VLESA's Q-filter makes the opposite, and for this application the more defensible, trade-off: 89.4% unsafe recall at some cost to raw safe-rate optimism.
- **Intrinsic classification quality (Table 2, EgoSafety test set, isolating the Q-filter from upstream noise)**: VLESA's GRPO-trained filter reaches 89.8% accuracy with balanced recall (90.2% safe / 89.4% unsafe), a 24.7-point accuracy gain and 54.2-point unsafe-recall gain over the zero-shot prompting filter (65.1% accuracy, 95.0%/35.1% safe/unsafe recall). This is presented as the mechanistic explanation for the downstream ASIMOV-2.0 gap: the prompting filter's low unsafe-recall directly causes its low real-world intervention accuracy.
- **Ablation — intent–action predictor backbone (Table 4)**: swapping Llama-4-Scout for Llama-4-Maverick changes VLESA's Δt=0 accuracy only modestly (67.2%→63.0%) and its Safe-Rate gain moderately (41.3→26.4), whereas the prompting baseline is far more fragile to this swap (28.0%→30.2% accuracy but its ΔSR collapses 40.4→17.7). The paper reads this as evidence the GRPO-trained Q-filter is robust to variation in upstream candidate quality, while the prompting filter's usefulness is much more tightly coupled to which model it's paired with.
- **Full Pareto sweep (Appendix C.2, Table 5)**: confirms the same pattern holds across the entire Δt ∈ {0, 0.5, ..., 3.0}s × K ∈ {1,3,5} grid — VLESA's front and the baseline's front never cross.

## Limitations (as stated by the authors)

From §5, stated by the authors themselves:

- **Evaluation relies on synthetic or curated data.** ASIMOV-2.0-Video is generated with VEO3 (a video generation model), and EgoSafety's unsafe half is VLM-generated rather than collected from real unsafe events. The authors explicitly say "robustness on genuine streaming egocentric video with real sensor noise and long-tail hazards remains unverified" and flag collecting real unsafe egocentric footage as a natural next step.
- **Fundamentally intent-dependent, with no correction mechanism for wrong goal inference.** Because safety is evaluated *against the inferred goal*, a wrong goal estimate cascades directly into a wrong safety judgment. The Q-filter is a learned classifier with "no formal guarantee" and, per Table 2, still misses roughly 10% of unsafe actions; the authors suggest confidence calibration and pairing with reachability-style formal verification as possible mitigations, not implemented here.
- **Coverage bounded by design choices.** Actions are restricted to the fixed EASG-derived vocabulary (219 verbs / 407 objects / 16 relations), and only K sampled candidate futures are screened per timestep — so an out-of-vocabulary or simply unpredicted hazard is structurally unreachable by the intervention mechanism. The authors also note latency limits applicability "in fast-evolving settings," and suggest broader action representations and more efficient backbones as future work.

## Critical read

**What's genuinely novel**: The central idea — making safety evaluation *explicitly conditioned on an inferred latent goal*, so that one safety model generalizes across arbitrary tasks without retraining, instead of building a safety classifier tied to one fixed policy/task distribution — is a clean and fairly convincing reframing, and the paper is careful to explain why this differs from a standard RL value function `Q^π(s,a)`. The data-generation trick (editing scene-graph triplets on a fixed frame to synthesize unsafe *labels* rather than rolling out unsafe *videos*) is a genuinely useful piece of engineering: it sidesteps a real bottleneck (naturally-occurring unsafe egocentric footage is rare) cheaply, at the cost of being only as good as the vocabulary-constrained, VLM-imagined notion of "plausible unsafe variant." The framing of GRPO training as producing better-*calibrated* recall balance (vs. a prompted model's systematic bias toward predicting "Safe") is a clear, well-supported empirical story, not just an assumed benefit of RL post-training.

**What's incremental**: GRPO itself is used essentially off-the-shelf (the same recipe as DeepSeekMath/DeepSeek-R1, applied to a binary VQA reward rather than math correctness) — the contribution is in the training data and task framing, not in modifying the RL algorithm itself. Likewise, the "constrained decoding" step is a fairly simple weighted re-ranking formula (Eq. 6) combining a rank-based prior with a safety penalty — effective, but not conceptually new relative to any score-combination re-ranker. The overall system architecture (a frozen big VLM for perception/proposal + a small fine-tuned specialist model for scoring/filtering) is also a well-established pattern in the broader "VLM + safety filter" literature the paper itself cites (e.g., latent-space safety filters, VLM-in-the-loop steering).

**Points that could be overstated or need more scrutiny**:
- The comparison that produces the headline "+41 percentage point" and "doubles intervention accuracy" numbers is against a **same-architecture, zero-shot prompting baseline** built by the authors themselves, not an existing published system — a reasonable and clearly-labeled ablation for isolating the effect of GRPO post-training, but readers should note the "prompt-based baseline" is not a prior work, it's the authors' own control condition.
- **ASIMOV-2.0-Video contains only unsafe videos** (transitions from safe to unsafe states) — the paper explicitly notes this when describing the EgoSafety classification comparison. This means the headline intervention-accuracy numbers say nothing about the false-positive rate on genuinely safe, uneventful footage; a monitor that is somewhat trigger-happy would still score well on this benchmark. The EgoSafety test-set numbers (balanced Safe/Unsafe) partially address this, but the two benchmarks are testing different things and it's worth not conflating "89.8% balanced accuracy on EgoSafety" with "89.8% accuracy in general deployment."
- The claim that the prompting baseline's higher raw Safe Rate is "only an artifact" of its safe-label bias is a reasonable interpretation given the recall breakdown, but it's presented with more certainty than the single-dataset evidence fully establishes — it's a plausible and well-supported story, not a proven causal mechanism.
- There's a small unresolved inconsistency in the reported constrained-decoding threshold (τ=0 in the main text's Eq. 6 and Algorithm 1, vs. τ=0.5 in the Implementation paragraph of §4.1) that isn't explained; it doesn't appear to change the qualitative results, but a reader trying to exactly reproduce the pipeline would need to check the released code rather than the paper text alone.
- The 32-rule "robot constitution" and the EASG vocabulary are both borrowed wholesale from prior work (Sermanet et al. 2025 for the constitution; Rodin et al. 2024 for EASG) — this is reasonable reuse, but it also means the paper's notion of "unsafe" is entirely downstream of how comprehensive and well-calibrated those external artifacts are; the paper doesn't audit or critique the constitution's coverage itself.

**Open questions the paper leaves for future work (largely matching its own stated list)**: validation on real (not synthetic/VLM-generated) unsafe egocentric footage; formal-verification-style guarantees or confidence calibration layered on top of the learned Q-filter to address the ~10% miss rate; broadening the action vocabulary beyond the fixed EASG schema; latency/efficiency improvements for genuinely fast-evolving real-time settings.

## Background: Borrowed Concepts

**Robot Constitution** — Sermanet, P., Majumdar, A., Irpan, A., Kalashnikov, D., & Sindhwani, V. (2025). *Generating Robot Constitutions & Benchmarks for Semantic Safety.* arXiv:2503.08663. [Ref 5]
A "robot constitution" is a set of natural-language rules (in the spirit of Asimov's laws, but far more granular and context-specific) that specifies what a robot or embodied agent should and shouldn't do — covering things like harm prevention, hazard awareness, and appropriate conduct — expressed as plain-English statements rather than a hard-coded logical or geometric constraint. The originating work frames this as a way to make safety "semantic": instead of specifying safety as a fixed region of state space to avoid (as in classical control), safety is defined by whether an action, in context, would violate one of these natural-language principles — which is precisely the judgment call VLESA's Q-filter is trained to make. VLESA borrows the 32-rule constitution wholesale (reproducing it in Appendix A) rather than designing its own. My knowledge of this specific paper is general rather than detailed — I know the core framing (natural-language rules as a safety spec, generated/refined for LLM-based judgment) but not the precise methodology by which the originating authors generated or validated their rule set, so treat this as a conceptual gloss rather than a summary of their exact approach.

**Control Barrier Functions (CBFs)** — foundational control-theory concept; cited via Ames, Grizzle & Tabuada (2014) and Liu & Tomizuka (2014). [Refs 6, 7]
A Control Barrier Function is a scalar function `h(x)` defined over a system's state space, chosen so that the set of states where `h(x) ≥ 0` is exactly the "safe set" you want the system to stay inside — the zero level set (`h(x)=0`) is the safety boundary itself. The key mathematical property enforced is *forward invariance*: as long as the system starts safe, the control input at every instant is constrained (typically via a small optimization problem solved online) so that `h(x)` can never decrease fast enough to cross zero, which guarantees the system can never leave the safe set even under worst-case dynamics within the model's assumptions. VLESA borrows only the *sign convention and framing* from this idea — its learned `Q_φ` mimics a CBF's zero-boundary structure (negative = safe, positive = unsafe) — but explicitly does not inherit the formal forward-invariance guarantee, since `Q_φ` is a learned classifier over images and language rather than an analytically verified function over known system dynamics. I have solid, standard textbook-level knowledge of CBFs, so this description should be reliable.

**Hamilton-Jacobi (HJ) Reachability** — foundational control-theory concept; cited via Bansal et al. (2017), Yang et al. (2025), Li et al. (2025), and Fisac et al. (2019). [Refs 8, 9, 20, 21]
HJ reachability analysis answers a different but related question from CBFs: given a system's dynamics and a set of "bad" states to avoid, what is the complete set of starting states from which the system is guaranteed to be able to avoid disaster, even under worst-case disturbances — and what's the best control policy to achieve that? This is computed by solving a Hamilton-Jacobi partial differential equation backward in time, whose solution is a value function whose sign indicates whether a state is safe (analogous to a Q-function: negative/positive value marks safe/unsafe), and whose gradient at the safety boundary gives the optimal safety-preserving control action. VLESA's paper cites this lineage ("following Q-based safety filters from control") as the conceptual ancestor for treating safety evaluation as a Q-valued function at all — the idea of learning a Q-like function whose sign directly encodes a safety verdict descends from this literature, even though VLESA's actual `Q_φ` is a fine-tuned VLM classifier with no PDE-solving or formal guarantee behind it. My knowledge here is reasonably solid on the conceptual framing (value function sign = safety verdict, computed via backward-reachability) but more approximate on the specific technical contributions of the individual cited works (e.g. the "multiplicative Q-networks" variant in Ref 20), so take the general framing as reliable and the specifics of those individual papers as unverified.
