Planning with Diffusion for Flexible Behavior Synthesis

Janner, Du, Tenenbaum & Levine · ICML 2022 · arXiv:2205.09991

Diffuser is a diffusion probabilistic model trained directly on whole state-action trajectories, so that planning becomes almost the same operation as sampling from the model: generate a plan by iteratively denoising a noisy trajectory, optionally nudged by a guide function toward high reward or toward satisfying constraints like a goal location. Tested on long-horizon sparse-reward maze navigation, multi-task goal reaching, block-stacking under novel goal configurations, and the D4RL offline locomotion benchmark, plus a warm-starting trick for faster planning.

Most planning systems learn a model of the world, then hand it to a separate search algorithm — and that search algorithm often finds and exploits the model's mistakes. This paper trains one model that does both jobs: it builds a whole plan by starting from random noise and gradually cleaning it up, the way photo-restoration software sharpens a blurry image, optionally nudged toward a goal or a reward along the way. It's tested on mazes, block-stacking, and standard robot-locomotion benchmarks, plus a trick for making the cleanup process faster.

Reading map

The paper's arc: it opens by arguing that "learned model + classical trajectory optimizer" rarely works, because optimizers exploit the model's blind spots. Its fix is a generative model of entire trajectories, so planning and sampling become nearly identical. Background sets up trajectory optimization and diffusion-model basics; Section 3 is the actual method (architecture, reward-guided sampling, goal-conditioning via inpainting); Section 4 catalogs the resulting properties; Section 5 tests each one; Section 6 places the work in context.

Here's the shape of the whole paper: it starts by pointing out that combining a learned world-model with a classic planner tends to backfire, because the planner finds the model's weak spots. The fix is one model that both understands the world and generates the plan, so there's no separate planner left to exploit anything. Then it works through what that model looks like, what neat side-effects fall out of the design, and tests each one on a different task.

§1

Abstract

Role in the paper States the core claim and result in miniature.

P1

Model-based RL typically uses learning only to fit an approximate dynamics model, then hands the rest of the work to a classical trajectory optimizer — a combination the authors argue has real empirical shortcomings. Their alternative folds as much of the optimization pipeline as possible into the model itself, via a diffusion probabilistic model that plans by iteratively denoising trajectories; classifier-guided sampling and image inpainting are reinterpreted as planning strategies, and the framework is evaluated in long-horizon, test-time-flexible control settings.

Most robot/agent "planning" systems work in two separate steps: learn a model of how the world reacts, then hand that model to a separate optimizer that searches for a good sequence of actions. This paper trains one model that does both jobs at once — a model that generates a whole plan by starting from random noise and gradually "cleaning it up" into something sensible, the way noise-reduction software sharpens a blurry photo. It works well on tasks that need long-range thinking and the flexibility to handle new goals on the fly.

§2

1 Introduction

Role in the paper Motivates the problem, states the alternative, introduces Diffuser and its four headline properties.

P2

Planning with a learned model is appealing because it confines learning to what it's best at — approximating unknown dynamics f as a supervised problem — after which the model plugs into well-understood classical trajectory optimizers (Tassa et al., Posa et al., Kelly).

The appeal of "learn the world, then plan with a normal planner" is that you only ask the learning part to do the thing learning is good at — predicting what happens next.

P3

In practice this rarely works: powerful optimizers exploit inaccuracies in the learned model, producing plans that resemble adversarial examples more than good trajectories (Talvitie; Ke et al.). As a result, most model-based RL leans on model-free machinery (value functions, policy gradients) instead of the trajectory-optimization toolbox, and methods that do plan online tend to fall back on simple gradient-free routines (random shooting, cross-entropy method) to dodge the exploitation problem.

The catch: search algorithms are *too good* at finding weaknesses. Handed a slightly-wrong learned model, they'll find the exact cracks in it and produce a "plan" that only looks good to the flawed model — like finding a glitch in a video game rather than actually winning it. So in practice people mostly avoid strong search methods and use only very cautious, simple ones.

P4

The paper's alternative: train a model directly amenable to trajectory optimization, so sampling from it and planning with it become nearly identical. This reframes design priorities — action distributions matter as much as state dynamics, long-horizon accuracy matters more than single-step error, the model should stay reward-agnostic so it transfers across tasks, and its *plans* (not just its predictions) should improve with experience and resist the myopic failure modes of shooting-based planners.

The paper's alternative: instead of training a world-model and bolting a planner on afterward, train a single model that's already shaped like a good plan-generator, so "asking the model for a sample" and "asking the model to plan" become the same request. That shift changes what the model should be good at — nailing the whole future trajectory matters more than nailing just the next step, and the model shouldn't be tied to one specific goal, so it can be reused for new goals later.

P5

This is instantiated as Diffuser, a trajectory-level diffusion probabilistic model (Sohl-Dickstein et al.; Ho et al.) that predicts all timesteps of a plan simultaneously rather than autoregressively. Its iterative sampling process gives flexible conditioning: auxiliary guides can modify sampling to recover high-return or constraint-satisfying trajectories.

Concretely, this becomes Diffuser: a model that guesses an entire plan all at once (not one step at a time), refining it through repeated rounds of "de-noising," the same trick used by AI image generators. Because it builds the plan gradually, you can nudge it partway through toward what you want, the way you might guide a sketch as it's being drawn rather than only judging it once finished.

P6

Long-horizon scalability — — Diffuser is trained for whole-trajectory accuracy rather than single-step error, so it avoids the compounding rollout error of single-step dynamics models and scales better with planning horizon.

Long-horizon scalability — — because it's graded on the whole plan rather than one step at a time, small errors don't pile up the way they do for models that only ever predict one step ahead.

P7

Task compositionality — — reward functions supply auxiliary gradients during sampling, so multiple rewards can be combined simply by summing their gradients.

Task compositionality — — different goals can be combined just by adding their "nudges" together, like layering multiple sets of directions onto the same map.

P8

Temporal compositionality — — global coherence emerges from iterated local consistency, letting Diffuser generalize by stitching together in-distribution subsequences into novel trajectories.

Temporal compositionality — — by only ever enforcing "does this look locally sensible," the model can mix and match pieces of different training routes into a brand-new route it never saw.

P9

Effective non-greedy planning — — because model and planner are the same training procedure, improving predictions also improves planning, yielding a planner that handles long-horizon, sparse-reward problems that trip up conventional planners.

Effective non-greedy planning — — since practicing to predict well *is* practicing to plan well, the model naturally avoids painting itself into a corner the way short-sighted planners do.

P10

The core contribution is this denoising diffusion model for trajectory data plus its probabilistic framework for behavior synthesis — unconventional relative to standard deep model-based RL, but shown to be effective in offline control requiring long-horizon reasoning and test-time flexibility.

In short: an unusual-looking model for this field, but one that appears to genuinely help with long, sparse-reward tasks and last-minute changes of goal.

§3

2.1 Problem Setting

Role in the paper Formalizes trajectory optimization so Section 3 has fixed notation to build on.

P11

A system evolves as s_{t+1} = f(s_t, a_t). Trajectory optimization seeks the action sequence a*_{0:T} maximizing an objective J factored over per-timestep rewards: a*_{0:T} = argmax_{a_{0:T}} J(s_0, a_{0:T}) = argmax Σ_t r(s_t, a_t), with T the planning horizon. τ = (s_0, a_0, ..., s_T, a_T) denotes an interleaved state-action trajectory, and J(τ) its objective value.

This is just vocabulary-setting: the world moves from one state to the next based on an action, and "trajectory optimization" means picking the whole sequence of actions that racks up the most reward over time. τ is just shorthand for "one full run": every state and action, in order.

§4

2.2 Diffusion Probabilistic Models

Role in the paper Supplies the generative-modeling machinery Diffuser is built from.

P12

Diffusion models pose data generation as an iterative denoising procedure p_θ(τ^{i-1}|τ^i), the reverse of a forward process q(τ^i|τ^{i-1}) that progressively corrupts data with noise. The induced data distribution is p_θ(τ^0) = ∫ p(τ^N) Π_i p_θ(τ^{i-1}|τ^i) dτ^{1:N}, with p(τ^N) a standard Gaussian prior and τ^0 the (noiseless) data. θ is fit by minimizing a variational bound on negative log-likelihood; the reverse process is typically parameterized as Gaussian with fixed timestep-dependent covariances, p_θ(τ^{i-1}|τ^i) = N(τ^{i-1} | μ_θ(τ^i,i), Σ^i), and the forward process is prespecified rather than learned.

Take a clean photo (or trajectory) and blur it more and more, step by step, until it's pure noise — that's the "forward" process, and it's fixed in advance, not learned. The model's whole job is to learn to run that tape backward: starting from noise and un-blurring it, one small step at a time, until a clean, plausible example pops out.

P13

Notation. — Two "times" are in play: diffusion timestep (superscript i) and planning timestep (subscript t). s^0_t is the t-th state of a noiseless trajectory; when unambiguous, the superscript-0 is dropped (τ = τ^0). τ_{s_t} and τ_{a_t} denote the t-th state/action within τ.

Notation. — There are two different "step counters" running in this paper — one for how far along the noise-removal process you are, and one for how far along the actual plan (which point in time) you're looking at — the paper just needs two different labels to keep them straight, this paragraph is purely bookkeeping.

§5

3 Planning with Diffusion (introduction)

Role in the paper Bridges Background into the actual method by naming the core trick — perturbing the trajectory distribution instead of bolting a planner onto the model.

P14

Trajectory optimization needs the dynamics f, which learning-based methods normally approximate and then feed to a conventional planner — but learned models are often poorly matched to planners designed around ground-truth dynamics, so planners end up exploiting the model.

Same complaint as before, restated right before the method: you need to know how the world works to plan well, but a slightly-wrong learned "how the world works" model gets exploited by strong planners.

P15

The proposed fix: subsume as much of planning as possible into the generative model pθ(τ) itself, so planning ≈ sampling. Diffusion's iterative denoising lends itself to flexible conditioning via a perturbed distribution p̃_θ(τ) ∝ p_θ(τ) h(τ) (Eq. 1), where h(τ) encodes prior evidence, desired outcomes, or objectives to optimize (reward/cost). Sampling from this perturbed distribution is a probabilistic analogue of the Section 2.1 trajectory-optimization problem — trajectories that are both physically plausible under p_θ(τ) and high-reward/constraint-satisfying under h(τ). Because dynamics information (p_θ) is separated from the task-specific perturbation (h), one diffusion model can be reused across multiple tasks in the same environment; the rest of Section 3 works out Diffuser itself, then two instantiations of h(τ) — a reinforcement-learning analogue of classifier guidance, and goal-conditioning as inpainting.

The core move: instead of layering a planner on top of the model, bias the model's own sampling process toward what you want. Think of the un-blurring process as being willing to take a hint — "lean toward outcomes that look like this" — where the hint can be a goal, a reward, or some known facts about the start of the plan. Because the hint is kept separate from the base model, you can reuse the same base model with a different hint for a different task, the way you might reuse the same camera with different filters.

§6

3.1 A Generative Model for Trajectory Planning

Role in the paper Specifies Diffuser's architecture from four design constraints.

P16

Temporal ordering. — Blurring model and planner forces giving up autoregressive-in-time prediction: goal-conditioned inference p(s_1 | s_0, s_T) needs the next state to depend on a future state as well as a past one. Dynamics prediction is causal (present determined by past), but decision-making can be anti-causal (present decisions conditioned on the future) — so Diffuser predicts all timesteps of a plan concurrently rather than autoregressively.

Temporal ordering. — Normally, predicting the future means going strictly forward in time. But planning sometimes needs a prediction that depends on both the past *and* a desired future ("get from here to that goal") — so instead of guessing step-by-step in order, the model guesses the entire plan at once.

P17

Temporal locality. — Despite not being autoregressive or Markovian, Diffuser keeps a relaxed temporal locality: each denoising step (implemented as a single temporal convolution) only has a receptive field over nearby timesteps, so any one step enforces only local consistency — but composing many such steps drives global coherence of the sampled plan.

Temporal locality. — Each little clean-up step only looks at a small neighborhood of nearby moments in the plan — like a photo restoration tool that only touches one small patch of the image at a time. Do that enough times, over enough overlapping patches, and the whole picture ends up coherent even though no single step ever saw the whole thing.

P18

Trajectory representation. — Because the quality of the resulting controller matters as much as the quality of state predictions, states and actions are predicted jointly — actions are just extra state dimensions. Diffuser's input/output is a 2D array τ = [[s_0,...,s_T],[a_0,...,a_T]] (Eq. 2), one column per horizon timestep.

Trajectory representation. — The model doesn't just guess "what will happen" (states) — it guesses "what will happen and what you'll do about it" (states and actions) together, side by side, like two rows in the same spreadsheet, one column per moment in time.

P19

Architecture. — Three constraints follow: predict the whole trajectory non-autoregressively, keep each denoising step temporally local, and allow equivariance along the horizon dimension but not the state/action-feature dimension. This is satisfied by a U-Net of repeated temporal-convolutional residual blocks — the same family that works for image diffusion models, but with 2D spatial convolutions replaced by 1D temporal convolutions (Figure A1). Because it's fully convolutional, the planning horizon is set by input dimensionality, not architecture, so it can vary dynamically during planning.

Architecture. — The recipe: predict the whole plan at once, only fix up nearby moments at each step, and treat "which moment in time" differently from "what feature of the state." That recipe is built the same way state-of-the-art image-generation models are built, just swapped from 2D pictures to a 1D strip of time. A nice side effect: since the network doesn't care in advance how long the plan is, you can ask for a longer or shorter plan without retraining anything.

P20

Training. — Diffuser parameterizes a learned noise-gradient ε_θ(τ^i, i), from which the reverse-process mean μ_θ has a closed form (Ho et al.). Training minimizes the simplified objective L(θ) = E[||ε − ε_θ(τ^i, i)||²], with i ~ Uniform{1,...,N} the diffusion timestep, ε ~ N(0,I) the noise target, and τ^i the clean trajectory τ^0 corrupted by ε. Reverse-process covariances Σ^i follow the cosine schedule of Nichol & Dhariwal.

Training. — Skippable at the plain-language level beyond: the model is trained to guess "how much noise was added" to a corrupted plan and get better at removing exactly that noise — this paragraph is mostly the specific math recipe for that (a loss function and a noise schedule) with no further conceptual payload beyond "train it to reverse the corruption."

§7

3.2 Reinforcement Learning as Guided Sampling

Role in the paper Turns "solve an RL problem" into "sample conditionally from the diffusion model," using the control-as-inference framework.

P21

To introduce reward, the paper appeals to control-as-inference: let O_t be a binary optimality variable per timestep with p(O_t=1) = exp(r(s_t,a_t)). Setting h(τ) = p(O_{1:T}|τ) in Eq. 1 gives p̃_θ(τ) = p(τ | O_{1:T}=1) ∝ p(τ) p(O_{1:T}=1|τ) — the RL problem has become a conditional-sampling problem.

To bring in the idea of "good" vs "bad" outcomes, the paper imagines a coin-flip at every moment that comes up "optimal" more often when the reward there is high. Wanting a good trajectory then just means asking the model: "generate me a trajectory, but only from among the ones where every one of those coin flips came up optimal."

P22

Exact sampling from this distribution is intractable, but when p(O_{1:T}|τ^i) is sufficiently smooth, the reverse transitions can be approximated as Gaussian (Sohl-Dickstein et al.): p_θ(τ^{i-1}|τ^i, O_{1:T}) ≈ N(τ^{i-1}; μ+Σg, Σ) (Eq. 3), where μ, Σ are the original reverse-process parameters and g = ∇_τ log p(O_{1:T}|τ)|_{τ=μ} = Σ_t ∇_{s_t,a_t} r(s_t,a_t)|_{(s_t,a_t)=μ_t} = ∇J(μ) — i.e., the guide is the gradient of total return evaluated at the current mean.

That "only the good ones" request can't be answered exactly, but under reasonable assumptions it works out to nudging each clean-up step in the direction that increases total reward the fastest — mathematically, nudge by the gradient of the return. This paragraph is largely the derivation showing that nudge is well-defined; the conceptual takeaway is just "steer each denoising step slightly uphill in reward."

P23

This directly parallels classifier-guided image generation (Dhariwal & Nichol): first train p_θ(τ) on all available trajectory data's states and actions, then train a separate model J_φ to predict a trajectory sample's cumulative reward; J_φ's gradients guide sampling by shifting the reverse-process means per Eq. 3. The first action of a sampled τ ~ p(τ|O_{1:T}=1) is executed in the environment, then planning restarts — a standard receding-horizon control loop, given as Algorithm 1.

This is exactly the same trick used to make AI image generators produce "a cat" on demand (train a separate classifier, then follow its gradient) — here the "classifier" is trained to predict how good a trajectory will turn out, and its gradient steers the plan toward high reward. In use: generate a plan, take its first action, then throw the plan away and regenerate — repeating that loop is the actual control strategy (Algorithm 1).

§8

3.3 Goal-Conditioned RL as Inpainting

Role in the paper Supplies the second instantiation of h(τ), for constraint-satisfaction rather than reward-maximization goals.

P24

Some planning problems are better posed as constraint satisfaction — find any feasible trajectory satisfying constraints such as ending at a goal location — than as reward maximization. Given the 2D array representation of τ, this becomes an inpainting problem: state/action constraints play the role of observed pixels, and the diffusion model must fill in every unobserved array location consistent with them.

Some tasks aren't "maximize a score," they're "just get me anywhere that satisfies these requirements" — like "end up at this exact spot." That's handled the same way image-editing AI fills in a torn or missing patch of a photo: you already know some pixels (the start and the goal), and the model fills in everything in between.

P25

The needed perturbation is a Dirac delta on observed values and constant elsewhere: for state constraint c_t at timestep t, h(τ) = δ_{c_t}(s_0,a_0,...,s_T,a_T), effectively +∞ where s_t = c_t and 0 otherwise (action constraints identical). In practice this is implemented by sampling from the unperturbed reverse process τ^{i-1} ~ p_θ(τ^{i-1}|τ^i) and then overwriting the sampled values with the conditioning values c_t at every diffusion timestep i ∈ {0,...,N}.

Mechanically: wherever you already know the value (say, the goal state), just force the model's guess to equal that known value at every stage of clean-up, and let the model fill in everything else naturally. This is a specific, mostly-mechanical implementation detail rather than a new idea — the concept is already covered by "inpainting" above.

P26

Even pure reward-maximization needs conditioning-by-inpainting, because every sampled trajectory must start at the current state — this is what line 10 of Algorithm 1 enforces.

Even ordinary reward-chasing plans need a bit of this "known pixel" trick, because every plan has to start from wherever the agent actually is right now — that's just forcing the very first entry of the plan to match reality.

4 Properties of Diffusion Planners

Role in the paper Names and empirically motivates (via Figure 3) the emergent behaviors the design is meant to produce.

P27

This section discusses properties distinctive to Diffuser relative to standard dynamics models or unusual for non-autoregressive prediction.

This section is a tour of the useful side-effects of the design choices already described — plain-language versions already appear at P28–P31.

P28

Learned long-horizon planning. — Single-step models are dynamics proxies decoupled from any particular planner; Diffuser's planning routine (Algorithm 1) is tightly coupled to diffusion sampling itself — planning differs from sampling only by the guidance h(τ) — so Diffuser's strength as a long-horizon predictor transfers directly to long-horizon planning (Figure 3a shows feasible trajectories in sparse-reward settings where shooting methods struggle; quantified in Section 5.1).

Learned long-horizon planning. — Because "planning" here really is just "sampling, with a nudge," Diffuser inherits whatever the underlying model is good at — and it's good at long sequences — so it plans well over long horizons where reward is sparse (imagine finding your way through a maze that only rewards you at the very end).

P29

Temporal compositionality. — Single-step models lean on the Markov property to compose in-distribution transitions into novel ones; Diffuser achieves an analogous generalization non-Markovially, by iteratively improving local consistency (Section 3.1), letting it stitch familiar subsequences into new trajectories. Figure 3b: trained only on straight-line trajectories, it generalizes to V-shaped trajectories by composing two at their intersection point.

Temporal compositionality. — Trained only on trajectories going straight one way or straight another way, the model can still produce a route that goes one way *then* the other — stitching together pieces it's seen separately into a shape it's never actually seen as a whole, the way you might combine two familiar shortcuts into a new route.

P30

Variable-length plans. — Because Diffuser is fully convolutional along the horizon dimension, planning horizon isn't fixed by architecture — it's set by the dimensionality of the initializing noise τ^N ~ N(0,I), allowing variable-length plans at inference (Figure 3c).

Variable-length plans. — Because the model doesn't have a fixed idea of "how long is a plan," you can just ask for a longer or shorter one at the moment you use it, without retraining — like a printer that can print any page length instead of being locked to one size.

P31

Task compositionality. — Diffuser encodes dynamics and behavior but stays independent of reward function, acting as a prior over futures; planning can then be steered by comparatively lightweight perturbation functions h(τ) (even sums of several), demonstrated by planning for a reward function unseen during diffusion-model training (Figure 3d).

Task compositionality. — The base model doesn't know or care what the goal is; goals are supplied afterward as light "nudges," so the exact same trained model can chase a brand-new goal it's never seen, just by handing it a new nudge.

5 Experimental Evaluation (introduction)

Role in the paper States the three capabilities under test plus a runtime study.

P32

Experiments target: (1) planning over long horizons without manual reward shaping, (2) generalizing to new goal configurations unseen in training, and (3) recovering an effective controller from heterogeneous, variable-quality offline data — concluding with a study of practical runtime/performance tradeoffs.

The tests are designed to answer three questions: can it plan far ahead without hand-holding, can it adapt to new goals it wasn't trained on, and can it learn something useful from messy, mixed-quality past data? Plus: how much can you speed it up?

5.1 Long Horizon Multi-Task Planning

Role in the paper Tests long-horizon, sparse-reward planning and multi-task goal transfer.

P33

Maze2D environments (Fu et al.) require reaching a goal location worth reward 1, with no shaping elsewhere; reaching it can take hundreds of steps, which even the strongest model-free baselines struggle to credit-assign correctly (Table 1).

In a maze where you only get rewarded for reaching the exit — nothing for getting closer — it can take hundreds of moves to get there, and that's exactly the kind of task where typical learning methods lose track of "which of my hundreds of moves actually mattered."

Competing methods hold up decently when the exit never moves, but degrade noticeably once the exit starts moving around; and giving a competing method the perfect map of the maze (no learning error at all) still doesn't fix its long-horizon planning problem, which is the point.

§12

5.2 Test-time Flexibility

Role in the paper Tests generalization to novel goal configurations via block-stacking, with a single trained model reused across settings.

P36

A block-stacking suite covers Unconditional Stacking (build as tall a tower as possible), Conditional Stacking (a specified block order), and Rearrangement (match reference block locations in a novel arrangement), trained on 10,000 PDDLStream demonstration trajectories with reward 1 on successful placement, 0 otherwise.

Three block-stacking challenges: stack as high as possible, stack in a specific order, or rearrange blocks to match a target layout — all learned from 10,000 example demonstrations.

P37

One Diffuser model serves all three settings, changing only h(τ): Unconditional Stacking samples from the unperturbed p_θ(τ) to emulate the demonstration controller; Conditional Stacking and Rearrangement compose two perturbations — one maximizing likelihood of matching the goal final state, one enforcing an end-effector/cube contact constraint (Appendix B).

One single trained model handles all three challenges; only the "nudge" changes between them. For the harder challenges (specific order, specific layout), two nudges are combined at once: "look like the target arrangement" and "keep the arm actually in contact with the block while stacking."

5.3 Offline Reinforcement Learning

Role in the paper Tests single-task control quality on the D4RL locomotion benchmark against a broad set of prior methods.

P39

Using the D4RL offline locomotion suite (Fu et al.), Diffuser's sampled trajectories are guided toward high reward (Section 3.2) and conditioned on the current state via inpainting (Section 3.3); the reward predictor J_φ is trained on the same trajectory data as the diffusion model.

On a standard set of robot-locomotion benchmarks built from pre-recorded (not live) data, Diffuser is steered toward high-reward routes and anchored to start from the robot's actual current position, the same two tricks described earlier.

P40

Baselines span model-free RL (CQL, IQL), return-conditioning (Decision Transformer), and model-based RL (Trajectory Transformer, MOPO, MOReL, MBOP).

It's compared against a wide range of other approaches to learning from that same kind of offline data.

P41

Diffuser performs comparably overall (Table 2) — beating MOReL, MBOP, and Decision Transformer, but behind the best techniques purpose-built for single-task performance. A separate experiment using Diffuser purely as a dynamics model inside conventional MPPI performed no better than random, which the authors read as evidence that Diffuser's effectiveness comes from coupled modeling-and-planning, not from superior open-loop predictive accuracy.

Diffuser lands in the middle of the pack here — better than several methods, a bit behind the very best specialist methods for this particular kind of task. 5.4 Warm-Starting Diffusion for Faster Planning

Role in the paper Addresses Diffuser's main practical limitation — slow iterative plan generation.

P42

Individual plans are slow to generate due to iterative denoising, and naive open-loop execution requires regenerating a full plan at every step.

The one real downside so far: generating a plan takes many small clean-up steps, and if you regenerate a brand-new plan from scratch at every single moment of acting in the world, that's slow.

P43

Warm-starting instead runs a limited number of forward-diffusion steps from the previous plan, then a matching number of reverse (denoising) steps to regenerate an updated plan. Figure 7 sweeps this denoising-step count from 2 to 100 on Walker2d Medium-Expert, showing the planning budget can be cut markedly — down to roughly a tenth — for only a modest performance drop, provided each new plan is initialized from the prior timestep's plan.

The fix is to not start from scratch each time — reuse yesterday's plan, lightly re-noise it, and only run a few clean-up steps to update it, rather than the full process. This turns out to save a lot of time (cutting the work down to roughly a tenth) while barely hurting performance, as long as you're updating an existing plan rather than generating a fresh one.

6 Related Work

Role in the paper Situates Diffuser against prior model-based RL and prior diffusion-model applications.

P44

Deep generative modeling has reached model-based RL via dynamics models parameterized as convolutional U-nets, stochastic recurrent networks, VQ-autoencoders, neural ODEs, normalizing flows, GANs, energy-based models, graph neural networks, neural radiance fields, and Transformers (extensive citation list), plus non-autoregressive trajectory-level dynamics models (Lambert et al.). All of these generally assume an abstraction barrier between model and planner — learning approximates dynamics, then any planner or policy-optimization method can be plugged in independently. This paper's goal is to break that barrier by training model and planner together as one non-autoregressive trajectory-level model where sampling and planning are nearly identical.

Plenty of prior work has used fancy learned models for planning, but nearly all of it keeps model and planner as two separate, swappable pieces. This paper's whole point is to stop separating them.

P45

Other lines of work break the same abstraction barrier differently: autoregressive latent-space models for reward prediction, weighting model-training objectives by state values, and collocation techniques applied to learned single-step energies. In contrast, this method plans by modeling and generating all timesteps of a trajectory concurrently and conditioning samples via auxiliary guidance functions.

A handful of other papers have also tried blurring that same line, just via different technical routes; this paper's route is generating the entire plan at once and biasing it afterward, rather than any of those alternatives.

P46

Diffusion models generally are framed as an iterative denoising data-generating process; the denoising step can be read as parameterizing the data distribution's gradients, linking to score matching and energy-based models. Iterative gradient-based sampling naturally supports flexible conditioning and compositionality, which this paper leverages to recover behavior from heterogeneous data and to plan for unseen reward functions. Diffusion models have previously targeted images, waveforms, 3D shapes, and text, but — to the authors' knowledge — not reinforcement learning or decision-making before this work.

Diffusion models — the "gradually clean up noise" trick — had already proven themselves for pictures, audio, 3D shapes, and text by the time this paper came out; as far as the authors knew, nobody had yet pointed that same trick at robot planning or decision-making. That's the gap this paper fills.

§16

7 Conclusion

Role in the paper Restates the contribution and its practical upshot.

P47

Diffuser makes planning almost identical to sampling from a trajectory-level diffusion model, differing only via auxiliary perturbation functions that guide samples. This buys graceful handling of sparse rewards, the ability to plan for new rewards without retraining, and temporal compositionality (producing out-of-distribution trajectories by stitching in-distribution subsequences) — pointing, in the authors' framing, to a new class of diffusion-based planning procedures for model-based RL.

The headline idea in one sentence: planning and sampling become basically the same thing, with a small nudge as the only difference — and that turns out to buy graceful handling of sparse rewards, easy adaptation to new goals, and the ability to remix familiar pieces into new routes.
